   An increase in angle of attack of a planes’ wing results in an increase in both lift and induced drag (skybrary.aero, n.d.). If the angle of attack is too steep, the airflow across the upper surface of the aerofoil gets disconnected. This results in the airflow around the wing not being laminar (Al Bowers, 2011) resulting in a loss of lift, also known as stalling. The angle at which relative wind strikes an aerofoil is known as the angle of attack. The relative velocity between the aeroplane and the atmosphere is represented by the angle created by the aerofoil's chord and the relative wind direction (skybrary.aero, n.d.). By finding ways to increase the angle of attack, we can reduce the time taken for take-off and landing which would reduce the fuel consumption as a huge amount of fuel is needed for that part of the journey.
